The context

An insurance policy for the reef

Over 50% of the Great Barrier Reef’s coral cover has been lost in recent decades. Bleaching events in 2016, 2017, 2020 and 2022 caused catastrophic damage to the northern and central sections. Climate change is accelerating the frequency and severity of these events, and conventional reef restoration — coral transplantation — while vital, is too slow to keep pace with the rate of loss.

The Forever Reef Project applies a different logic: the same principle that underpins seed banks for terrestrial plants, applied to coral genetics. Collect and preserve living specimens and genetic material from every coral species in the Great Barrier Reef before they’re permanently lost. If a species is destroyed by bleaching, the biobank holds the genetic material to potentially reintroduce it when conditions allow.

The project

The world’s first living coral biobank

The Forever Reef Project is housed at a purpose-built facility at the Port Douglas Superyacht Marina in North Queensland — the world’s first publicly accessible living coral biobank. Led by Great Barrier Reef Legacy, a not-for-profit built on 35+ years of reef expedition and research experience, the project aims to collect living specimens of all 400 coral species in the Great Barrier Reef and preserve them in a ‘coral ark’.

The facility currently houses over one third of the reef’s coral species. Living fragments, tissue samples and DNA are made available for reef research and restoration efforts globally. The project works closely with Traditional Owners — inviting collaboration with all 72 clan groups along the Great Barrier Reef — and has established First Nations Coral Hubs for Indigenous-led engagement and stewardship of Sea Country.

Dr Charlie Veron — known as the ‘Godfather of Coral’ and former Chief Scientist at the Australian Institute of Marine Science, who has discovered over 20% of the world’s coral species — has described this as “without question, the most important project we can be undertaking for corals and coral reefs.”

The role

Advisory board member since 2025

I joined the advisory board of Great Barrier Reef Legacy in 2025, bringing product strategy, digital, and commercial expertise to an organisation doing some of the most important conservation science in Australia. Working alongside the team on digital engagement, fundraising strategy, and the commercial partnerships needed to sustain and scale the Forever Reef facility and its collecting expeditions.
